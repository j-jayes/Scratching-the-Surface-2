Scratching the Surface

Classical CV vs Vision-Language Models for steel defect detection

Jonathan Jayes

2026-05-18

A. Hook

The 30-second pitch

How can we prepare for the unknown?

Is computer vision a solved problem?

How can we save time with new tech?

The goal of today’s talk is to help us think about preparing for a project when we don’t have all of the information that we need from the client.

For AQ Group one of the tasks that we could work on was rolled-metal defect detection. This is a problem that has been worked on for decades, and there are many classical computer vision techniques that can be used to solve it. However, there are also new vision-language models that have been shown to be very effective at a wide range of computer vision tasks.

So the question is, how can we use this new technology to save time and resources on a project like this? And how can we prepare for the unknowns that we might encounter along the way?

C. What do we know from AQ Group?

What is their setup??

We just don’t know what the setup at the various AQ group factories is. Ideally, we would have a team of engineers who have set up a high quality imaging camera system that is capable of capturing high resolution images of the rolled-metal as it is being produced, as in the top left quadrant of the image. They would also have a team of data annotators who have labeled a large dataset of images with the various types of defects that can occur, as in quadrants two and three. This would allow us to train a supervised learning model to detect the defects in real-time as the metal is being produced. I think this is unlikely to be the case, because if they had gone to all of the work of putting in the cameras and annotating the data, they probably would have already trained a model to do this, and celebrated the completion of a successful project, as in the bottom right quadrant of the image. So I think it’s more likely that we will have to work with a less than ideal setup, which is something that we will have to prepare for.

We make some assumptions about the project spec

We will treat the problem as if they have

  • no labelled data,

  • but want us to as quickly as possible provide them with a system that can identify defects on the production line.

Then in this demo, I choose

  • two datasets that have some similarities to what we might expect from AQ Group as training data

  • and use another dataset as a holdout set to test how well our models generalise to new domains.

D. Is computer vision a solved problem?

Yes — classical CV is a (mostly) solved problem!

The standard CV taxonomy: classification, detection, segmentation.

The people who solved it

…and the network that started it

AlexNet (2012) — the architecture that ended hand-crafted feature engineering and started the deep-learning era of computer vision.

Talk about the progression in Computer vision, from Mnist to ImageNet to the present day. Talk about how resnet and yolo models have very fast inference, but need the images that they are trained on to be very similar to the ones that they predict on. They are good at identifying the patterns that they have seen in the training data, but they are not good at generalizing to new domains. This is a problem for AQ group, because we don’t know what the domain of the images that we will be working with is. We might be working with images that are very different from the ones that we have seen in the training data, and this could lead to poor performance.

But the issue is when we change the domain…

The annotation bottleneck

Classical CV runs on labels.

Every time the camera moves, the lighting changes, or a new defect class shows up, the whole pipeline goes back to the annotator queue.

For AQ Group we don’t yet know:

  • which mill
  • which camera, which lighting rig
  • which defect taxonomy matters in production

So the safe bet is a system that can start without labels.

The problem is that we will have to spend a lot of time speaking to engineers and asking them to annotate data, and this is a bottleneck that we want to avoid. We want to be able to start working on the problem without having to wait for the annotations to be done, and this is where vision-language models come in.

Datasets at a glance

All labels collapsed to a single binary defect / no-defect target. The two TRAIN POOL datasets are what ResNet50 and YOLO11s see during training. KolektorSDD2 is the new mill — different camera, different lighting, different defect vocabulary — that the classical models have to generalise to.

The point of the table is to be concrete about what the models do and do not see. Severstal + NEU-DET ≈ 11 700 labelled images of two specific imaging setups. KolektorSDD2 is reserved as the held-out domain: same task (“is there a defect?”), totally different pixels. NEU-DET ships all-defect (no normals) — that’s what a lot of real factory data looks like before anyone has bothered to annotate a clean baseline.

The domain-gap problem, visualised

Same task — “is this strip defective?” — wildly different pixels.

This is the visceral “look-and-feel” of distribution shift. We didn’t change the task — we changed the camera.

  • Different sensors, illumination, exposure, magnification
  • Different defect taxonomies (scratches vs silk-spots vs welding lines)
  • Different aspect ratios (1600×256 vs 229×645)
  • Different surface treatments

A classifier trained on the top row has never seen anything that looks like the bottom row.

Two classical workhorses

Two architectures we’ll lean on. ResNet50 is the canonical image classifier — convolutional backbone, pooled features, one sigmoid output. YOLO is the canonical real-time detector — same kind of backbone, but the head predicts a grid of bounding boxes with class scores. ResNet gives us “is this strip defective?”. YOLO gives us “and here’s where, with a box per instance”. Both are pre-trained on ImageNet / COCO and then fine-tuned on Severstal + NEU-DET.

Why does the F1 collapse?

It’s not that the network “forgot how to see.” It’s that the features it learned to chase — strip-aligned texture statistics from Severstal — simply aren’t where the defects live in Kolektor.

The model is doing exactly what it was trained to do. The training distribution lied about the world.

No classical recipe gets us there.

Fine-tuning on Kolektor helps — F1 climbs from 0.16 to 0.56 — but still leaves us far short of production-ready.

Qualitative companion to the scoreboard. The F1 numbers don’t tell you what each output looks like. Walk the rows:

Row 1 (Severstal, in-domain): ResNet fires on the whole bright region (p̂=1.00) — useful as a yes/no, useless if you want to know which defect. YOLO drops four tight boxes on the actual scratches with per-instance confidence. That’s a work order for an operator.

Row 2 (Severstal, in-domain): Same story — three crisp boxes vs one broad blob.

Row 3 (Kolektor, OOD): Both models break, but differently. ResNet says “no defect” (p̂=0.11) and the CAM is on irrelevant texture. YOLO does fire — two low-confidence boxes — but they’re at the top, not on the actual scratch in the middle.

Neither is great on the OOD row. The point of the slide is different output shape, same domain-gap problem — bringing more classical machinery to bear doesn’t close the gap, it just changes what “failure” looks like. Time to try something different.

Slide goal: Highlight the fundamental architectural paradigm shift OpenAI went through in under 3 years.

  • GPT-3.5 & GPT-4 base — traditional, blind LLMs. Text-in, text-out. No fusion: there were no other modalities to handle.
  • GPT-4V & classic Voice — late fusion. Components stitched together; information is lost (tone of voice flattened to plain text; vision pre-filtered through a separate encoder). It’s a pipeline bottleneck.
  • GPT-4o onwards — early fusion. No translators or adapters: images and raw audio go straight into the core network alongside text. Enables human-level latency and native audio outputs.

G. Prompt-tuning as gradient descent on words

A structured-output teacher critiques a structured-output student

The loop (scripts/prompt_tune_vlm.py)

  1. Score base prompt on a 12-image calibration set
  2. Collect every disagreement with its parsed reasoning + attributes
  3. Feed disagreements to gpt-5.5 as a “domain prompt engineer”
  4. Tuner emits JSON: summary, common_mistakes[], prompt_addendum
  5. Append the addendum, re-score, repeat

The tuner output is schema-enforced — strict JSON, no free-form prose. The addendum becomes a # DOMAIN-SPECIFIC GUIDANCE block prepended to the student’s user prompt.

The tuner’s schema

TUNER_SCHEMA = {
    "type": "object",
    "properties": {
        "summary": {"type": "string"},
        "common_mistakes": {
            "type": "array",
            "minItems": 1,
            "items": {
                "type": "object",
                "properties": {
                    "pattern": {"type": "string"},
                    "fix": {"type": "string"},
                },
                "required": ["pattern", "fix"],
            },
        },
        "prompt_addendum": {
            "type": "string",
            "description": (
                "Markdown to append under '# DOMAIN-SPECIFIC GUIDANCE'. "
                "Explains the domain, what counts as a defect, and "
                "the visual heuristics that disambiguate the hard cases."
            ),
        },
    },
    "required": ["summary", "common_mistakes", "prompt_addendum"],
}

The “explain the domain” bit is what makes this generalise. We’re not just patching mistakes — the tuner has to teach the student what a hot-rolled steel strip is, what counts as a scratch vs. a silk spot, why oil residue isn’t a defect, etc.

I. The economics: when does a VLM pay for itself?

How fast is each model on one image?

  • Log scale
  • Linear scale

This is the latency reality check. A T4-served ResNet handles an image in ~6 ms — that’s ~160 images per second per GPU. The cheapest VLM in our bake-off (GPT-4.1-mini on Azure) takes ~3 seconds; Gemini-2.5-pro takes ~17. That’s three to four orders of magnitude. A 60 frames-per-second inspection line is fine for the classical model and impossible for the VLM. The point of the next slide isn’t to say VLMs are bad — it’s to say: that latency comes from somewhere, and someone has to pay for it. Flip to the linear tab to make the scale gap visceral.

…and what does each call actually cost?

  • Log scale
  • Linear scale

The latency gap shows up almost 1:1 as a cost gap. Running ResNet on a rented T4 costs fractions of a cent per thousand images — most of the bill is the VM, not the compute. Calling Gemini-2.5-pro on the same 1 000 images costs about $4.35 — roughly 5 000× more. GPT-4.1-mini sits in between at ~$0.83. So the choice isn’t “smart vs dumb model” — it’s “is the marginal accuracy from the VLM worth 4 to 5 orders of magnitude of cost?” Which sets up the break-even curve on the next slide.

Does the student ever catch the teacher?

Setup (scripts/labelling_curve.py)

  • Pool: Kolektor train (~2 300 images, ~10 % defective)
  • Labels: VLM pseudo-labels with measured noise (TPR=0.68, TNR=0.96)
  • 3 seeds per N — no class balancing
  • 6 epochs, AdamW, ResNet50

Reading the chart

  • At N ≤ 1 600, the student has not caught the teacher (F1 peaks ~0.37 vs 0.79).
  • The gap signals that more labels and/or a cleaner label budget would be needed.
  • The dollar axis makes the trade-off concrete: $10.74 of labelling still falls short.

The student (ResNet50) never catches the teacher (GPT-5.4 zero-shot) within the 1 600-image budget tested. This is an honest result — the teacher is very good and the labels are noisy. The practical implication is that you need either more images, better labels, or a fine-tuned VLM starting point before the classical model matches.

J. The takeaway

Use VLMs to teach classical CV. Don’t put them in the hot loop.

What we showed

  1. Classical CV fails under realistic domain shift — and Grad-CAM tells you why.
  2. Frontier VLMs generalise zero-shot to unseen mills and defect taxonomies.
  3. A prompt-tuning loop, with a structured-output teacher, moves F1 measurably on a 12-image calibration budget.
  4. VLMs and Grad-CAM give complementary explanations — where and why.
  5. At line-rate volumes, the break-even versus paid labelling lands in hours, not months.

The recipe for AQ Group

  • Day 1 — drop a VLM in shadow mode behind the existing camera. Zero labels needed.
  • Week 1 — harvest the VLM’s pseudo-labels. Tune the prompt against your disagreements.
  • Week 2 — train a small ResNet / YOLO on the pseudo-labels. Put that in the hot loop.
  • Ongoing — escalate the bottom-30%-confidence frames back to the VLM. Re-train weekly.

The VLM is the teacher and the safety net, not the production classifier.
